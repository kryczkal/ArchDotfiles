# Tight Prose

Write text so it builds the reader's understanding directly, without bureaucratic scaffolding, decorative language, or visual structure substituting for reasoning. Every sentence either moves the reader's mental model forward or links pieces of it; nothing else is allowed.

## The core insight

A text earns its right to exist by being the most efficient path of a specific understanding from writer to reader. Nothing more, nothing less. Every other principle in this skill is a consequence of that.

If a sentence doesn't move the reader's model — it's noise. If the structure fragments the reasoning into disconnected chunks — it's noise. If the language forces the reader to decode corporate phrasing or foreign-sounding calques — it's noise. Cut the noise, and what's left is the text.

## The meta-test

After reading the text, does the reader have a clearer mental model of the situation than before?

- Yes → the text did its job.
- No, or the model is muddier → the text failed, regardless of how polished it looks.

Every paragraph, every sentence, every bold, every metaphor, every number — evaluated by whether it contributes to that clearer model. If it doesn't, cut it.

---

## Philosophy — why we write this way

These five principles sit underneath everything else. The strategy and tactics below are consequences.

**Ph1. Text is a tool for a decision, not an artifact.** Know, before writing, what decision the reader needs to make after reading. Every sentence is evaluated by whether it moves the reader toward that decision. This eliminates "Let me be clear", "As mentioned above", "It's important to note" — those phrases don't advance the reader's model, they're the author clearing their own throat.

**Ph2. The reader is smart and busy.** Write as one intelligent person to another. Don't explain the obvious, don't over-justify, don't hedge every claim. Where something is obvious, half a sentence. Where something is contentious or novel, a paragraph. Attention is asymmetric.

**Ph3. Author and reader are peers in the same conversation, not supplicant and authority.** Don't beg. Don't over-promise. Don't flatter. State the plan, show the numbers, name the questions. Ask when you need an answer. Admit when you don't know. Position: a serious operator presenting a plan, not a junior hoping for approval. Confidence without arrogance.

**Ph4. The text is transparent to the idea.** The reader notices the idea, not the text. No rhetorical ornament, no stylistic performance, no phrases for their own sake. The language disappears and leaves: what, why, how much, when.

**Ph5. Every uncertainty is explicitly named.** Don't pretend to know more than you do. "If X happens..." "Assuming Y..." "Realistic range is A to B." Naming uncertainty builds trust — the reader sees what's an assumption versus what's known, and can reason alongside the author.

## Strategy — how a whole document is built

These are document-scale decisions. They shape the whole, not just individual paragraphs.

**St1. Orient the reader in concrete before going abstract.** The opening establishes who, when, where, what specifically — concrete anchors. Abstraction is built on that ground. If the first sentence of a doc is abstract, the reader has nothing to hook it onto; it floats. Ground them first, let the abstraction emerge.

**St2. Structure emerges from the reader's mental-model construction, not from a template.** Don't default to "Problem → Solution → Market → Team" or any rigid shape. Ask: what does the reader need to understand FIRST so that the SECOND thing makes sense? Order the doc that way. Each section should build on the previous.

**St3. Metaphors are structural choices, not decoration.** If you use a metaphor, it must map 1:1 onto the actual thing. If the doc has a central thesis sharp enough to support a metaphor (like a loop that actually is iteration, or tracks that actually are linear preparation) — use it and let the whole doc refer back. If it doesn't, don't force one. A strained metaphor is worse than none.

**St4. Asymmetric attention.** Space in the document is a resource. Give more of it to what matters more to the reader's decision. Things you handle competently in-house: half a sentence. Things that are contentious, load-bearing, or non-obvious: a paragraph. Don't spread attention uniformly.

**St5. Show through example, not through label.** A product is explained through dialogue of what it feels like to use, not through a feature list. A cost is shown through a specific number, not through "affordable." A strategy is shown through a concrete action, not through "we will prioritize." Labels are the writer's shortcut; examples are what convinces.

**St6. Conversational anchors are built into the structure.** Direct address to the reader ("Pytanie do Was", "one question for you") belongs in the structure, not as a footnote. Questions are not weakness — they're signals of partnership. The reader is inside the conversation, not watching it.

**St7. If the doc proposes action or asks for a decision, define the scope explicitly.** Where does this conversation end? What's outside it? "When X happens, we come back with Y" or "beyond this, it's a separate conversation." The reader knows what they're committing to and what they're not. (This doesn't apply to retrospectives, reports, or reference documents — only to action-proposing docs.)

## Tactics — sentence and paragraph mechanics

These are consequences of the philosophy and strategy above. They look like style rules but each one exists because of a deeper principle.

**T1. Every sentence carries meaning.** Each sentence is a fact, a reason (*why*), or a connector between ideas. Test: cut the sentence — is the argument weaker? If no, cut it. (Consequence of Ph1 — a sentence that doesn't move the reader's model has no place.)

**T2. Causality stays on the surface.** Every claim has its reason attached nearby — same sentence or the next one. "We need X, because Y." "We skip X, because we have the capacity in-house." The reader never has to guess why something is in the doc. Example: *"żeby iterować apkę pod realny feedback, musimy być blisko userów"* — the ask (be in SF) has its reason (iterate under real feedback) glued to it. (Consequence of Ph1 and Ph4.)

**T3. Paragraph is a coherent self-contained thought.** Premise → development → concrete anchor → conclusion. The "concrete anchor" is whatever makes the claim real: a number in a budget, a named example in an argument, a specific action in a plan. Anything that turns abstraction into something the reader can touch.

**T4. Numbers flow inside sentences.** Not **€9–13K** as a bold tag at the end of a paragraph. Instead: "on the first year it's about 9–13 thousand euros." The number is part of the argument, a clause in the sentence — not a transactional stamp.

**T5. Each unit (paragraph, bullet, section) ends with what the reader needs to walk away with from that unit.** For a bullet in a "what the money buys" list, that's the *purpose* of the spend category (costs live in tables). For a paragraph explaining a specific cost, that's the *amount*, inside a full sentence. For an argument, that's the *conclusion*. Don't end on a forgotten detail. And never end on a bold cost-tag that breaks prose flow.

**T6. Reasoning chains go as prose, not as bullets.** When implications chain (X therefore Y therefore Z), write the chain as a paragraph. The reader is led through continuously, not asked to reassemble fragments.

**T7. Visual hierarchy serves the prose, not replaces it.** Bold is fine for section openers and occasionally a key term the reader must notice. It's bad when it replaces prose — when an argument gets compressed into a bold label instead of being written out. Default is prose; bold is an accent that helps navigation, not a substitute for reasoning.

**T8. No calques.** A calque is a word-for-word translation from another language that reads as off-key to a native speaker. Test before every unusual phrase: would a native speaker actually use this in conversation? If no, rewrite. Calques break Ph4 — the reader stops on a weird phrase instead of moving through the idea.

**T9. Vocabulary matches how the reader talks with peers.** Don't be more formal than them — distances you. Don't be more casual — undercuts your seriousness. Same information, different register:
- Founder to tech-aware VC: "zbijamy infrę do zera przez cloud credits"
- CEO to government ministry: "minimalizujemy koszty infrastruktury dzięki środkom od dostawców"
- Engineer to engineer: "infra goes to zero via startup credits"
Content (numbers, specifics, causality) stays precise regardless of register.

**T10. Structure emerges from the narrative, not from meta-labels.** Don't pre-label with "Plan A:", "Nice-to-have:", "Hard ask:", "Structural question:". Let the prose carry structure. Section headers belong where the reader needs navigation across clearly distinct concerns — not inside a single line of reasoning.

## What this is NOT

Crucial for calibration — the style is adjacent to several others and can be confused with them.

- **Not blog writing.** Blog opens with personality beats ("Hey there!", "Let me tell you a story..."). This style is content-first, not personality-first.
- **Not marketing copy.** Marketing has aspirational framing ("Imagine a world where...", "We believe that..."). This style has no emotional pushes — just facts, reasons, numbers.
- **Not technical reference.** Reference material is complete and neutral; you look up what you need. This style is persuasive — it builds a case toward a decision.
- **Not executive summary with rigid template.** Exec summaries default to Problem/Solution/Market/Team. This style lets structure emerge from what the reader needs to construct in their head.
- **Not casual chat.** Chat is fragmented, incomplete, interruptible. This style has complete sentences and coherent argument arcs, just expressed in a casual register.

The closest positive description: **a tight operating update between two operators who already trust each other.** Casualness signals trust; precision signals competence.

## Method for rewriting existing text

1. **Identify the decision.** Before touching the text, know: what decision does the reader need to make after reading? If you don't know, find out first. Every rewrite choice flows from this.

2. **Read the whole thing once.** Understand the arc. Where does it start the reader, where does it leave them?

3. **For each paragraph, find the arc** — premise → development → concrete anchor → conclusion. Mark three kinds of problems:
   - **Filler sentences:** ones that only set up a list, repeat the heading, or do transition work ("Warto zaznaczyć...", "It is worth noting...", "Generally speaking..."). Cut them.
   - **Calques:** unusual phrasings that don't match how a native speaker would say this. Run the native-speaker test, rewrite.
   - **Fragmenting structure:** bold labels, sub-bullets, meta-headers that break what should be a single continuous arc. Collapse into prose.

4. **Rewrite each paragraph as continuous prose.** End each paragraph with what the reader most needs to walk away with from that paragraph.

5. **Check the whole against the meta-test.** Does the reader, after this version, have a clearer mental model than before? If not — what's missing, what's muddy?

6. **Calibrate register to audience.** Naturalize the vocabulary to how the reader talks with their peers. Tighten where still formal; formalize where gratuitously crude.

---

## Self-test before shipping

- After reading, is the reader's mental model of the situation clearer than before? (the meta-test)
- Does every sentence carry meaning — fact, reason, or connector? (no filler)
- Does causality live on the surface — every claim has its reason glued to it?
- Does each paragraph end with what the reader needs from that paragraph (purpose, conclusion, or the key number in a full sentence — never a bold cost-tag)?
- Would a native speaker of the target language actually use these phrases in conversation? (no calques)
- If you stripped all bold and em-dashes, would the prose still read as sensible argument? (structure lives in grammar, not layout)
- Does it sound like one smart person talking to another — not a corporate memo, not a marketing brochure, not a personality blog?

## Before / after examples

These are real paragraphs from a startup founder's mid-term budget document for a Polish VC. The style context: founder-to-investor Polish, casual-precise register, naturalized tech anglicisms. The same transformation principles apply in other languages and registers — only the specific phrasings shift.

### Example 1 — reasoning chain collapsed from "+"-chain into prose

**Before** (fragmented, "Plus do tego", long "+"-list, closes on cost-tag):
> **Podatki.** Spółka jest amerykańska, ale my jako osoby fizyczne mamy polskie konta i majątek — trzeba to raportować niezależnie od tego, gdzie siedzi spółka. Plus po 6 mies. w SF jesteśmy równolegle amerykańskimi rezydentami podatkowymi: deklaracja światowego dochodu + FBAR na polskie konta + obowiązkowy Form 5472 dla Delaware ($25K kary za brak) + deklaracja traktatowa żeby nie płacić podwójnie. Księgowy od obu stron, pierwszy rok: **~€9–13K**.

**After** (continuous arc, ends with conclusion inside a full sentence):
> **Podatki** — sprawa jest dwustronna. Spółka jest amerykańska, ale my zostajemy polskimi osobami fizycznymi z polskimi kontami i majątkiem, co trzeba rozliczać niezależnie od tego, gdzie siedzi firma. Po sześciu miesiącach w SF dochodzi do tego amerykańska rezydencja podatkowa — czyli deklaracja światowego dochodu, zgłoszenie polskich kont, obowiązkowe formularze dla Delaware (kara za brak pojedynczego jest rzędu 25 tys. dolarów) i deklaracja na bazie traktatu, żeby nie zapłacić podatku dwa razy. Samemu nie ogarniemy tego bez ryzyka błędu, więc bierzemy księgowego znającego obie jurysdykcje — na pierwszy rok to ok 9–13 tys. euro.

### Example 2 — calque removal + natural native phrasing

**Before** (calques: "Runway extension", "burn", "time to PMF", "najscarce resource", "shotów"):
> **5. Runway extension.** Każde €100K dokłada ~4 mies. horyzontu przy naszym burn. €500K → 18–24 mies. zamiast 12. Time to PMF jest najscarce resource — więcej czasu = więcej shotów na trafienie.

**After** (phrasings a native would actually use in this context):
> **Przedłużenie pasa startowego.** Każde 100 tys. euro ekstra daje nam ok cztery miesiące dłużej przy naszych miesięcznych kosztach. 500 tys. zamienia 12 miesięcy na 18–24. Czasu w pre-seedzie mamy najmniej ze wszystkiego — więcej czasu znaczy więcej prób i iteracji, więc większa szansa, że w końcu trafimy.

### Example 3 — de-labeled structure, causality surfaced

**Before** (meta-labels "Plan A" fragment the narrative; causality buried):
> **Wizy.** B-1 — zakazuje pracy dla US spółki, a my kodujemy 8h dziennie; wpadka = 5-letni ban.
>
> **Plan A**: celujemy w **E-2** (treaty investor): Wymaga Delaware z zainwestowaną kasą. **~$20K ≈ €18–22K** dla trójki; odnawialna w nieskończoność.

**After** (continuous argument, reason attached to claim, structure emerges from prose):
> **Wizy.** Żebyśmy mogli tam legalnie pracować, potrzebujemy czegoś więcej niż zwykłej wizy biznesowej — B-1 wprost zakazuje pracy dla amerykańskiej spółki, a my planujemy kodować po osiem godzin dziennie. Gdyby nas na tym złapali na granicy, dostajemy pięcioletni ban na wjazd. Realna ścieżka dla nas to E-2, czyli wiza traktatowa (Polska jest w tym traktacie od 2019). Wymaga tylko spółki w Delaware, do której wpłynie kasa z rundy — dla trójki kosztuje około 20 tys. dolarów, czyli 18–22 tys. euro, i jest odnawialna w nieskończoność.
